\section{The multilayer perceptron}
\label{sec:model}

\begin{definition}[Multilayer perceptron]
  \label{def:mlp}
  An $L$-layer multilayer perceptron with widths $d_0, d_1, \dots, d_L$ is the
  map $\mathrm{MLP}_{\vtheta} \colon \R^{d_0} \to \R^{d_L}$ given by
  $\vh_0 = \vx$ and
  \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:forward}
    \vh_\ell = \phi\!\left(\mW_\ell\, \vh_{\ell-1} + \vb_\ell\right),
    \qquad \ell = 1, \dots, L,
  \end{equation}
  with weights $\mW_\ell \in \R^{d_\ell \times d_{\ell-1}}$, biases
  $\vb_\ell \in \R^{d_\ell}$, and a nonlinearity $\phi$ applied elementwise. The
  output $\mathrm{MLP}_{\vtheta}(\vx) = \vh_L$ is left unactivated, and $\vtheta$
  collects every $(\mW_\ell, \vb_\ell)$.
\end{definition}

The nonlinearity is the only thing separating \cref{eq:forward} from a plain
linear model.
\Cref{fig:activations} plots the common choices; we use the rectifier
$\phi(x) = \max(0, x)$ throughout, which trained fastest in every run.
\Cref{fig:arch} shows the layered structure the definition produces.

That the model can fit essentially anything is classical.

\begin{theorem}[Universal approximation]
  \label{thm:ua}
  Let $\phi$ be continuous and sigmoidal. For every continuous
  $f \colon [0,1]^{d_0} \to \R$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$, there is a
  single-hidden-layer MLP $g$ with
  $\sup_{\vx \in [0,1]^{d_0}} \abs{f(\vx) - g(\vx)} < \varepsilon$.
\end{theorem}

\Cref{thm:ua} is due to \citet{cybenko1989} and was extended to essentially any
nonpolynomial $\phi$ by \citet{hornik1989}.
It is an existence result: it promises a network but bounds neither its width nor
what it takes to find it by gradient descent.
Counting the parameters shows how fast that width can cost.

\begin{proposition}
  \label{prop:params}
  The MLP of \cref{def:mlp} has $\sum_{\ell=1}^{L} d_\ell\,(d_{\ell-1} + 1)$
  parameters.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Layer $\ell$ contributes the $d_\ell \times d_{\ell-1}$ entries of $\mW_\ell$ and
  the $d_\ell$ entries of $\vb_\ell$, that is $d_\ell\,(d_{\ell-1} + 1)$. Summing
  over $\ell = 1, \dots, L$ gives the total.
\end{proof}
